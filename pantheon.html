The Olympians

Zeus

The deity of the sky, the weather, law and order, fate and destiny, and monarchy, Zeus was known as the King of the Gods. He was portrayed as a solidly built, regal, mature man sporting a thick beard. His standard attributes were an eagle, a royal sceptre, and a lightning bolt.

As the youngest of the Titans Kronos (Cronus) and Rheia, Zeus was called Zeus. Zeus avoided this fate when his mother whisked him away, giving the Titan a stone substitute wrapped in swaddling blankets, while Kronos devoured each of his newborns.

The deity was secretly nurtured on Mount Dikte in Krete (Crete), where he was fed by nymphs on the milk of the goat Amaltheia and protected by the warrior Kouretes (Curetes), whose shield-waving battle dance muffled the sound of the god's cries.

Zeus enlisted the goddess Metis in his cause when he reached adulthood. She provided the Titan Kronos with a sorcerous drink that made him vomit the young gods he had eaten.

From Tartaros' abyss, Zeus freed the six giant-sons of Heaven. As a token of appreciation, the Hekatonkheires (Hundred-Handed) provided him with lightning-bolts, while the Kyklopes (Cyclopes) supported him with boulder volleys to help him take on the Titanes. Ultimately, Kronos and his allies were vanquished and sent to an underground jail.

Zeus and his brothers cast lots to choose who would control the universe after the fall of the Titan gods. Zeus won the heavens, Poseidon the sea, and Haides the underworld.

When an oracle foretold that Metis's son would succeed him as King of the Gods, Zeus devoured the pregnant goddess. After that, Athena, their child, was born right out of his skull and into his belly.

Zeus married his sister Hera, ruler of the skies, after wooing her in the guise of a cuckoo-bird. However, it turned out that this union of the always arguing sky-god and sky-goddess was not meant to be!

Mankind was created by Prometheus, who also gave them fire that had been taken from the gods of heaven. Zeus ordered the birth of the first woman, Pandora, as retribution for this deed, and he sent her to Earth with a vessel full of problems to afflict humanity. An eagle was assigned to punish Prometheus, who was captured and bound to a mountain.

Zeus made the decision to exterminate the first human generations off the face of the planet by a massive deluge due to their descent into evil and depravity. Deukalion and Pyrrha, a good couple, were spared and later permitted to repopulate the earth by casting stones that turned into men.

Enraged at the Titanes' confinement, the earth goddess Gaia (Gaea) called for the Giants to rebel against the Olympos gods. They besieged the celestial stronghold, but Zeus' lethal lightning-bolts destroyed their ruler and numerous more.

Typhoeus, the most terrifying giant of his kind, was created by Gaia and placed atop Mount Olympos. The other gods scurried away in terror, and Zeus himself lost the fight when the monster tore the sinews from his limbs, making him immobile. Subsequently, Zeus vanquished the giant in a rematch after Pan stole his strength back, and Zeus confined the behemoth beneath Mount Etna.

Zeus enticed numerous mortal women, such as Antiope in the form of a satyr, Kallisto (Callisto) as the goddess Artemis, Danae as a shower of gold, Leda as a swan, and Europa as a bull.

Herakles, also known as Heracles, was the deity's favorite mortal son, supporting him through his tribulations and ultimately welcoming him to Olympos as a god.

For their impiety and transgressions against the gods, Zeus punished the most notorious villains in mythology, such as Tantalos for stealing ambrosia from heaven, Lykaon (also known as Lycaon) for feeding the gods human flesh, Ixion for attempting to rape Zeus' wife, the goddess Hera, and Salmoneus for attempting to pretend to be Zeus and stealing the worship that was rightfully theirs.

Minor Gods

Persephone

The Russian word "kokoshnik" is translated. In the past, it was used to describe a headgear worn by married Russian women in specific regions of the nation. The size and shape of these classic kokoshniks varied slightly. Some of them were enormous, as seen in the image on the left, while others were smaller. However, kokoshniks generally had the same fundamental crescent shape. Many of them had ribbons fastening them at the back of the head.

When the Romanov court started donning kokoshniks in the nineteenth century, they quickly gained popularity in the world of royal jewelry. To go with these classic Russian hat shapes, Russian tsarinas and grand duchesses copied the European tiara fashion, which had been popular at Napoleon's court in France. The huge kokoshnik headdresses that were a common feature of the traditional attire of many Russian peasants were typically transformed into smaller, crescent-shaped headdresses that resembled halos under the control of the Romanovs.

The Romanovs added jewels to their kokoshniks, sometimes giving tiaras firm fabric backings to give them the necessary shape, and other times having tiaras fashioned in the same style as kokoshniks so that no additional fabric was required. You can see how a row of pearls was attached to a diamond fringe tiara worn by Tsarina Maria Feodorovna in the detail of the photo at the top of this piece to resemble the crescent form of kokoshniks. Like the four daughters of Tsar Nicholas II, unmarried Russian grand duchesses typically wore plain fabric kokoshniks without significant gems, though pearls were occasionally added. Many grand duchesses added veils to their kokoshniks when they wore court attire.

Due in part to the Romanovs' many familial connections to other royal houses in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the kokoshnik-style tiara gained popularity across Europe. Queen Alexandra of the United Kingdom asked for a tiara modeled after one of Tsarina Maria Feodorovna's kokoshniks as an anniversary present in 1888. Her great-granddaughter, Queen Elizabeth II, still dons the same diamond-set white and yellow gold kokoshnik tiara [4]. Other royal and noble families also possess kokoshnik tiaras that were modeled after those worn at the Romanov imperial court, such as the Bernadottes in Sweden and the Rosenborgs in Denmark.

Because so many grand duchesses wed into different royal families, many examples of kokoshnik-shaped tiaras can still be found in European tiara collections today. Grand Duchess Olga Constantinovna of Russia, afterwards known as Queen Olga of the Hellenes, sent an emerald-studded kokoshnik to her new nation (see picture at right). The tiara has undergone changes throughout the years, but its current design still resembles its original kokoshnik design. As part of their wedding trousseaux, many Russian grand duchesses received diamond fringe tiaras that resembled kokoshnik headdresses. You can still see some of these tiaras on the descendants of these grand duchesses today in nations like Germany and Denmark (which they inherited from two Romanov duchesses, Grand Duchess Maria Pavlovna and Grand Duchess Elena Vladimirovna). Because of its ties to the Romanovs, these fringe tiaras with halo-like adornments have also been referred to as tiaras russe.

Other royals frequently bought kokoshnik tiaras from the Romanovs after the 1917 Russian Revolution. The spectacular Cartier sapphire and diamond kokoshnik tiara that Queen Marie of Romania purchased from her aunt, Grand Duchess Maria Pavlovna ("the Elder") of Russia, was owned by the Romanian royal family until Princess Ileana, Queen Marie's daughter, sold it in exile in the 1950s. Originally made for Grand Duchess Elisabeth Feodorovna, a member of the Romanov family who was assassinated by the Bolsheviks, and sold to the Yugoslavian king by her niece, Grand Duchess Maria Pavlovna ("the Younger"), Alexander I of Yugoslavia purchased the diamond and emerald kokoshnik for his new wife, Princess Maria of Romania, in 1922.

So, ask yourself a few straightforward questions if you want to simply identify a kokoshnik tiara today. Is it in the form of a half-moon or a halo? Is the top of it noticeably curved? Is it connected to Russia or was it influenced by Russia? Any "yes" responses to any of those inquiries indicate that you are most certainly looking at a kokoshnik.

The Pink Diamond Diadem

Designed by St. Petersburg jeweler Jacob David Duval for either Elizabeth Alexeievna (Princess Louise of Baden, 1779-1826), the wife of Tsar Alexander I, or Maria Feodorovna (Duchess Sophie Dorothea of W√ºrttemberg, 1759-1828). Whoever it was intended for, it is amazing that considering its age, it is still presentable and essentially unmodified.

The magnificent natural pink diamond, a 13.335 carat stone with more than 1,000 carats of diamonds surrounding it, is the focal point of the towering kokoshnik design. The stone was once foil-backed, which explains the variation in hue from image to image. The briolette-cut diamonds in the tiara's center are "en Tremblant," which means they are free to move, dangle, and toss prisms worthy of a disco ball around with each motion.

It is the only authentic diadem that has survived the revolution and is still in Russia. Over 1,200 small round cut diamonds and 175 large Indian diamonds totaling a triangular headband in the Empire style. Large diamonds in the shape of a tear are suspended freely in the middle row and shine brilliantly with every head movement. Due to the extraordinary pink diamond, experts value this work of art as being priceless. Empress Maria Feodorovna wore the pink diamond tiara during her wedding, and afterwards, other brides from the royal dynasty also wore it.

Given that it has the term "Russian Nuptial Tiara," it should be clear that the bride frequently wore it during royal marriages in Russia (along with a plethora of other imperial wedding jewels, including the wedding crown, because evidently one headpiece was insufficient). When Alexandra Feodorovna married Nicholas II in 1894, she was one of the last people to wear it (but not THE absolute last). The royal jewels were taken by the Bolsheviks after the assassination of the royal family in July 1918 and were collectively displayed in 1922. After that, many of the pieces were sold to affluent Americans and Europeans (more on that later). Some just vanished (like the wedding necklace made of diamonds). Additionally, some were kept by the Russian government and are secured in the Diamond Fund Museum at the Moscow Kremlin. And that is where the Russian Nuptial Tiara is currently located; it has been hidden there for almost a century.


The Pearl Pendant Kokoshnik

One of the magnificent Romanov artifacts that was taken into custody was the Pearl Pendant Kokoshnik Tiara. However, we can follow a paper trail that indicates where it ended up. At least for a time. However, let us go back to the beginning.

Bolin made the tiara in 1841 for Empress Alexandra Feodorovna, Nicolas I's wife, out of 25 of the finest pearls suspended from diamond arches. But the tiara was truly cherished by Marie Feodorovna, Tsar Alexander III's wife. So much so that she retained it in her home even though it was a piece of the Crown Jewels and not her personal tiara. In order to escape the eventual fate of her son and family (it is good to have family in high positions), Marie, by this point the Dowager Empress, fled to the Crimea during the Bolshevik revolution. King George V, her nephew, sent a ship to fetch her.

She had only her "day jewels" with her when she first escaped because she had no notion that she would never come back. When Prince Felix Yusupov (you may recall him as one of the assassins who killed Rasputin) went to her house to get the remainder of her jewelry collection, he discovered that they were already gone. The Bolsheviks had gained possession of it.

Contrary to many of the others, however (such as the Sapphire Wave Tiara, the Yugoslavian Diamond Kokoshnik Tiara, and Elizabeth Alexeievna's Diamond Kokoshnik Tiara), we can trace this tiara back to a Christie's auction in 1927 when it was purchased by jeweler Holmes & Co., who then sold it to the 9th Duke of Marlborough to give to his second wife Gladys.

When Gladys passed away, the tiara changed hands once more in 1978, making its way through the London jewelry trade before ending up in Imelda Marcos' collection. The tiara still rests in a vault at the Central Bank of the Philippines with countless other jewels that were purportedly bought with money from the Philippine government, awaiting their ultimate fate.

There have been rumors that the Philippine government will either exhibit the seized items, including The Russian Pearl Pendant Kokoshnik Tiara, to boost tourism or will auction them off.


The Yugoslavian Diamond Kokoshnik Tiara

First cousin to King Alexander, Prince Paul of Yugoslavia, wed Princess Olga of Greece and Denmark in 1923. Given that Olga was related to almost all of the significant royal families in Europe, this marriage was especially advantageous for Yugoslavia. Her mother was Grand Duchess Elena Vladimirovna of Russia, the daughter of the formidable and jewel-obsessed Grand Duchess Maria Pavlovna, the Grand Duchess Vladimir. Her father was Prince Nicolas of Greece.

Elena had received the customary jewel-encrusted bridal gifts fit for an Imperial Russian Grand Duchess, including this rumored Cartier-made Diamond Kokoshnik Tiara. On the occasion of her daughter Olga's wedding, she in turn gave her this tiara.

Tragically, Prince Paul took over as regent of Yugoslavia after King Alexander was killed in 1929. As a result of Queen Marie's retirement, Princess Olga assumed the role of first lady in practice.

Princess Olga wore her Diamond Kokoshnik for numerous official pictures and significant international events, including the Coronation ball of George VI, in addition to her Boucheron Tiara and her Ruby Olive Wreath Parure.

Prince Paul and Princess Olga fled Yugoslavia in 1945 and went into exile, but because Olga was closely related to other European royals who were still in power, the tiara was nevertheless visible at significant occasions. At her son's 1955 nuptials to the Italian Princess Maria Pia of Savoy, Princess Olga wore her kokoshnik.

The Sapphire Wave Tiara

When it comes to the Romanov crown jewels, there is a lot of mystery. But it stands to reason that we have long been aware of all of those mysteries. But in 2012, a brand-new puzzle was revealed. Several long-forgotten Romanov Jewels, including the Sapphire Wave Tiara, a magnificent diadem.

Let us begin the mystery's story at the very beginning. He was a mineralist and gemologist by the name of George F. Kunz, and it is in his honor that the gemstone "Kunzite" bears his name. George worked for the United States Geological Survey, an organization, when he passed away in 1932. At the age of 23, he also held a vice president position at Tiffany & Co. The secret was hidden away for many years in the USGS library in Reston, Virginia.

However, it was found in George's bequeathed papers in 2012 that there was a 1922 publication called The Russian Diamond Fund. Outside of being a book, the Russian Diamond Fund is a Kremlin-run institution that holds the remaining Russian Crown Jewels. But in 1922, not long after the last Tzar and his family had been killed by the Bolsheviks, the Diamond Fund was brimming with Romanov artifacts. And this publication has information about them. Interestingly, they were not included in Russia's Treasure of Diamonds and Precious Stones, a 1925 book that was thought to have the most thorough catalog of the Romanov Crown Jewels. Before now.

The Russian Diamond Fund was one of Kunz's copies, and inside it, researchers found four jewelry items that had never been published before. A sapphire bow brooch, an emerald and diamond necklace, a sapphire and diamond bracelet, and The Sapphire Wave Tiara. The tiara included nine seemingly impossibly huge sapphires that were encircled by wave-like diamond loops that each ended in diamond drops. It was so peculiar that some may even argue the tiara had an oceanic feel to it. The tiara was recognized as having belonged to Marie Feodorovna, who you may recall as Alexander III's wife and the mother of the last Tzar of Russia, in a drawing in Geoffrey Munn's book Tiaras: A History of Splendor.

The tiara was commonly worn backed by a traditional kokoshnik headpiece covered in cloth and capped with a row of diamonds (possibly from a diamond rivi√®re necklace). The painting to the right depicts an example of how the Tiara was worn by the Russian Imperial family. In addition to appearing in Konstantin Makovsky's photograph, Marie wore the tiara to the wedding of Prince Alfred, Duke of Edinburgh, and Grand Duchess Maria Alexandrovna of Russia in 1874. However, it remains a mystery as to where the tiara might be right now. The USGS research team was able to ascertain that the tiara and other diamonds, excluding the brooch, were lost to history after being sold at auction in 1927. They could have been disassembled, sold, and used to make other pieces by the Bolsheviks a long time ago.
